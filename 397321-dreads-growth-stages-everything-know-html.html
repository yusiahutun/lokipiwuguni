Dreads growth stages: Everything you need to know

Dreads, also known as locs, are now a popular hairstyle among men and women. Over time, they have surpassed the cultural and religious barriers that confined them to particular groups of people. Locs are perfectly suited for the kinky African hair. Like all other hairstyles, they require maintenance and products as they go through the different dreads growth stages.

If you are considering going the locs way, or have already begun, it is important to understand the different dreads growth stages. This will help you apply the right maintenance tips at each stage to help you achieve the best results with your hair. This could be the difference between enjoying healthy locs, or being thoroughly frustrated with them.

Dreads growth stages

Dreadlocks are a preferred hairstyle among many people due to their versatility and flexibility. However, at their initial stages, they require a lot of attention and high-maintenance.

Once you get through the early phases, you are bound to enjoy this look for a long period of time. Below are the loc stages by month. The stages of dreads pictures will guide you as you go through each phase.

Starter stage: 3-6 months

This starting phase is also referred to as the baby stage. At the starter stage, you get to create a parting pattern for your locs. It is advisable that the hair sections are not too small, as they can easily break. This is also the budding stage for locs, and they can form anywhere along the hair strand.

Some of the characteristics of the locs at this point are that they are small in size, neat, and have clean parts. The growth of the hair at this point depends on the hair type. To maintain the locs during this phase, try to wait at least two weeks before your first shampoo wash.

It is also advisable to use residue-free shampoo when you wash your hair and to keep the locs moisturized by applying conditioner daily. Also, do not de-entangle your dreads, and let them grow without disturbance.

Budding stage: 6-12 months

The budding stage is also known as the sprouting phase. How long does it take for locs to bud? The period of time taken is determined by the hair type, as some locs grow faster than others. The original partings should be maintained to allow each strand to be established as the hair spins. It is also crucial to ensure that enough holding product is used during re-twisting.

''How do you tell if your locs are growing?'' You will notice the new growth being puffy and swelled up. The coils will also start showing some thickness. This is an exciting phase for dreadlocks' growth as many people look forward to having long, flowing dreads.

During shampooing and retwisting, less of the hair coils become undone. This makes it easier to groom the hair. At the budding stage, it is important that you practice a light retwisting routine, which helps promote new growth. Avoid overdoing it, as this can lead to hair thinning and breakage.

Teenage stage: 12-15 months

One of the common questions for people curious about their dreadlock progression timeline is, ''How long should dreads be after a year?'' Due to the different hair types, it is not easy to give a specific length that should be achieved within 12 months.

The teen stage also referred to as the ''Ugly stage'' or the ''locking stage'' is characterized by locs that are fully formed, and are double the original size. The hair is not too long, and not too short, which is why some people refer to the locs as ugly at this point. One way to manage this is to have them all held together, and also be patient with your hair.

As the hair plumps and develops in form, you may feel like it is not growing lengthwise. This dreadlocks shrinking stage is because the locs are becoming denser and are tangling on the inside. One important maintenance tip at this level is to experiment using different pressure hairstyles to control some swelling. Also, wash and moisturize your hair regularly.

Mature phase: 15-18 months

The mature stage is characterized by your locs being long enough to lie flat or hang down. They are firm and are not reforming much anymore. Looser hair textures take longer to get to this point. Hence, they require a little more patience. The time your hair takes to get here also depends on your hair care regimen.

The dreads, at this point, are also thick enough to support themselves and do not require to be re-twisted often. They continue to grow in size and length. You can continue with your established regular shampooing and conditioning routine. Also, use the right products to prevent buildup and unravelling.

Rooted phase: 18-21 months

This final stage is also known as the adult stage and comes after several years. The locs hang differently and feel heavier and puffier. They have regularized in size. The dreads are pretty long at this point, and you could trim them if you prefer a manageable size.

Its important, even at this rooted phase to keep the hair hydrated, especially using natural oils. You should also maintain a regular deep conditioning routine, preferably every two weeks. Regular trimming is encouraged to control deterioration at the ends of the strands.

It is also advisable that you wrap your locs at night using a satin scarf to prevent breakage and dryness. Finally, dry your locs thoroughly to prevent bad odour.

If you have just begun the locs journey, much patience is required as you get through the various dreads growth stages. Hair types are different, and each one has its own pace of progression. You will attain the best results if you apply the recommended maintenance tips at each stage, and consult closely with your loctician.
