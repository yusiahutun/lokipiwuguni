Kenya Shilling Suffers Slight Loss Against US Dollar on Rising USD Demand

  • The Kenya shilling has experienced a decline against the US dollar at the start of the trading week, with noticeable fluctuations in its value
  • On April 15, the shilling opened at KSh 127.7 and closed the day at KSh 130.9, marking a significant drop in value within a single trading day
  • Despite minor fluctuations on April 16, the shilling continued to weaken, closing at KSh 131.6 by the end of the day

Elijah Ntongai, a journalist at TUKO.co.ke, has more than three years of financial, business, and technology research expertise, providing insights into Kenyan and global trends.

The Kenya shilling has suffered a loss against the dollar in the first two days of the trading week that began on April 15.

On Monday morning, April 15, the Kenya shilling traded at KSh 127.7 and closed the day at KSh 130.9.

There were slight ups and downs on April 16, and the shilling closed the market at KSh 131.6 on Tuesday.

These changes have been reflected in the forex market, with forex bureaus in Nairobi exchanging the dollar for KSh at 133 on April 17.

Satellite Forex Bureau in Nairobi reported that the market witnessed an increased demand for US dollars on April 16.

"There was a huge demand for dollars in the market today," @Satelliteforex reported on X.

Why there is demand for dollars in Kenya

Speaking to TUKO.co.ke, FXPesa leading market analyst Rufas Kamau expressed concerns that since Kenya imports many goods, payments for these imports may take more dollars from circulation, leading to increased demand.

"Imports will sooner or later scoop the new dollars in the markets, and we will be back to the same problem of dollar shortage. With US inflation rising to 3.5% in March, the US Federal Reserve will likely hold rates higher for longer, which could strengthen the dollar further. This is expected to help the dollar recover against the Kenya shilling," Kamau told Tuko.co.ke.

Zimbabwe's God-backed currency stronger than Kenya shilling

In other related news, Zimbabwe Gold (ZiG) showed a positive performance in the first trading week, appreciating 1.1% against the US dollar.

ZiG was introduced to replace the old Zimbabwean dollar, which had been severely impacted by inflation.

Reserve Bank of Zimbabwe governor John Mushayavanhu launched the currency on April 5, marking a significant step towards resolving the country's currency crisis.
