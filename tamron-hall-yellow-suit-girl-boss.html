Tamron Halls bright yellow suit is the girl boss look everyone needs for spring

Stop everything. We need to talk about Tamron Hall’s dreamy yellow suit. When the Tamron Hall Show host strolled out Tuesday, flashing a smile in a neon yellow Alex Perry Suit, we applauded like we were in the audience. 

We know Tamron will give us a statement-making style moment daily on her show (Vogue did dub her the best-dressed woman in daytime TV), but this suit was the girl boss inspo we were looking for to kick off the new year. 

Tamron Hall stunned in this yellow Alex Perry suit

Neon for winter that can carry over into spring? Yes, please. The structured, stretch cape blazer, comes complete with padded shoulders and pockets on the chest and side, and the stretch cape trousers have a high-rise fit and flared leg.

The sleek suit from the fashion designer’s Resort 21 collection can be purchased as separates, but even with a hefty price tag of $3,200 it’s already selling out everywhere.

The suit also comes in a bright purple colorway that we love too. That one is also selling fast. 

Tamron completed her look with a black top and gold Jennifer Miller earrings, and even added a pop of pizzaz with her shoe game in chunky, leopard-print Gianvito Rossi heels. 

We have a thing for pops of color so we fell in love with this look, and we feel strongly that it’s an essential trend for spring girl boss gear - even if you’re handling your work duties from home. For now, budget-friendly neon suits are scarce, so keep an eye on this trend to pop up in the next couple of months.

This is just the latest time Tamron made us swoon over her suit. In December, she wore a black and white lace Michael Kors collection suit that we wanted in our closets immediately. The daytime TV stunner paired her Michael Kors lace flare pants and matching blazer with a fitted black top, gold hoops, and two thin gold necklaces.

Tamron wearing a lace Michael Kors suit

As for what inspires her daytime TV style, Tamron told Vogue, “The fashion on the show is expressive.” She continued, “t relates to the freedom of having a show where anything can be discussed. [My audience and I] can laugh together, cry together, and have these rich conversations."
